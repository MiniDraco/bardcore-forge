## 🎹 Harmony & Tonal Materials

### The Raw Material: Notes & Pitch

**12 chromatic notes** — Western music divides the octave into 12 equal semitones: C, C♯/D♭, D, D♯/E♭, E, F, F♯/G♭, G, G♯/A♭, A, A♯/B♭, B → back to C.
**Semitone (half step)** — smallest standard interval (C→C♯). **Whole tone (whole step)** — two semitones (C→D).
**Sharp (♯)** raises a note a semitone; **flat (♭)** lowers it; **natural (♮)** cancels. **Double sharp (𝄪)** / **double flat (𝄫)** shift two semitones.
**Octave** — same note name at double the frequency (A2=110Hz, A3=220, A4=440); 12 semitones apart, sounds "the same but higher."
**Enharmonic equivalents** — one pitch, two spellings: C♯=D♭, F♯=G♭, A♯=B♭. Spelling depends on key/function (in E major it's D♯, not E♭).
**No black key** between E–F and B–C — those pairs are already a semitone apart.

### Intervals

**Interval** — distance between two pitches, named by number (size) + quality. Qualities: **P** perfect, **M** major, **m** minor, **A** augmented, **d** diminished.
- Perfect-able intervals: unison, 4th, 5th, octave. Major/minor-able: 2nd, 3rd, 6th, 7th.
- **Augmented** = a semitone wider than P/M; **diminished** = a semitone narrower than P/m.
- **Inversion rule**: number sums to 9 (3rd↔6th), and M↔m, P↔P, A↔d.

| Interval | Semitones | Sound / Feel | Famous Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Perfect unison | 0 | Identical, solid | — |
| Minor 2nd | 1 | Tense, dissonant, menacing | *Jaws* theme |
| Major 2nd | 2 | Gentle step, hopeful | "Happy Birthday" (1st leap) |
| Minor 3rd | 3 | Sad, dark, soulful | "Greensleeves" |
| Major 3rd | 4 | Bright, happy, stable | "Oh When the Saints" |
| Perfect 4th | 5 | Strong, open, heroic | "Here Comes the Bride" |
| Tritone (A4/d5) | 6 | Unstable, evil, restless | *The Simpsons* ("The Sim-") / "Maria" |
| Perfect 5th | 7 | Powerful, hollow, stable | "Twinkle Twinkle" / *Star Wars* |
| Minor 6th | 8 | Yearning, bittersweet | "The Entertainer" / "Go Down Moses" |
| Major 6th | 9 | Warm, sweet, open | "My Bonnie" / NBC chime |
| Minor 7th | 10 | Bluesy, tense, restless | "Somewhere" (*West Side Story*) |
| Major 7th | 11 | Sharp longing, dreamy | "Take On Me" (chorus leap) |
| Perfect octave | 12 | Bright, resolved, open | "Somewhere Over the Rainbow" |

**Consonant** — unisons, octaves, P5, P4, M/m 3rds & 6ths (stable, restful). **Dissonant** — m2, M2, M7, tritone (tense, want to resolve). → Stack consonances for calm beds; lean on tritone/m2 for dread or jazz bite.

### Scales

**Scale** — an ordered set of pitches spanning an octave; defines the tonal "palette" of a piece.

| Scale | Formula (steps) | Mood / Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Major** | W–W–H–W–W–W–H | Bright, happy, resolved. Pop/folk default. (C: C D E F G A B) |
| **Natural minor** (Aeolian) | W–H–W–W–H–W–W | Sad, serious, dark. (A: A B C D E F G) |
| **Harmonic minor** | W–H–W–W–H–**Aug2**–H | Exotic, tense; raised 7 gives a strong V. (A: …F G♯) |
| **Melodic minor** | up: W–H–W–W–W–W–H; down: natural | Smooth ascent (raised 6&7), jazzy. |
| **Major pentatonic** | 1 2 3 5 6 | Open, folk, universal, no half-steps. (C: C D E G A) |
| **Minor pentatonic** | 1 ♭3 4 5 ♭7 | Rock/blues solos, can't sound "wrong." (A: A C D E G) |
| **Blues** | minor pent + ♭5 | Gritty, vocal, soulful. (A: A C D **E♭** E G) |
| **Chromatic** | all 12 semitones | Tension, color, passing tones. |
| **Whole-tone** | all whole steps (6 notes) | Dreamy, floating, ambiguous (no tonic). Debussy/dream FX. |
| **Diminished / Octatonic** | alternating W–H or H–W (8 notes) | Tense, symmetrical, horror/jazz over dim7. |

→ Suno prompts: "pentatonic riff," "bluesy minor pentatonic guitar solo," "whole-tone dreamy interlude," "harmonic minor exotic melody."

### The 7 Modes (quick recap)

| Mode | Brightness | One-word mood | Signature note | Genre feel |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Lydian** | brightest | dreamy/magical | ♯4 | Film, prog |
| **Ionian** (major) | bright | happy | natural | Pop, folk |
| **Mixolydian** | warm-bright | bluesy/groovy | ♭7 | Rock, funk, Celtic |
| **Dorian** | neutral | hopeful-sad | ♮6 (in minor) | Jazz, folk, funk |
| **Aeolian** (nat. minor) | dark | sad | ♭6 | Pop ballad, metal |
| **Phrygian** | darker | Spanish/menacing | ♭2 | Flamenco, metal |
| **Locrian** | darkest | unstable | ♭2 & ♭5 | Rare, jazz/metal color |

→ Brightness order (bright→dark): Lydian → Ionian → Mixolydian → Dorian → Aeolian → Phrygian → Locrian. Each step flattens one more note.

### Circle of Fifths

**Circle of fifths** — the 12 keys arranged by ascending 5ths (C→G→D→A→E→B→F♯→C♯/D♭→A♭→E♭→B♭→F→C). Clockwise adds sharps, counter-clockwise adds flats.
- **Order of sharps**: F C G D A E B ("Father Charles Goes Down And Ends Battle"). **Order of flats** = reverse (B E A D G C F).
- **Key-signature trick**: last sharp is the leading tone (one below tonic); second-to-last flat names the key.

| Sharps | Key | Flats | Key |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | C | 0 | C |
| 1 | G | 1 | F |
| 2 | D | 2 | B♭ |
| 3 | A | 3 | E♭ |
| 4 | E | 4 | A♭ |
| 5 | B | 5 | D♭ |
| 6/7 | F♯/C♯ | 6/7 | G♭/C♭ |

**Relative keys** — major & minor sharing a key signature (C major ↔ A minor; relative minor = 6th degree / 3 semitones down). Same notes, different tonal center.
**Parallel keys** — same tonic, different mode (C major ↔ C minor). Used for mode-mixing/borrowed chords.
**How to USE it:**
- **Modulation** — move to a neighboring key (adjacent on circle = 1 accidental difference = smooth). C→G or C→F is easiest.
- **Borrowing chords** — neighbors share most chords; pull a chord from the parallel/relative for color.
- **Diatonic chord-finding** — the I, IV, V of a key sit adjacent on the circle.

### Chords (built by stacking 3rds)

**Triad** — 3 notes, stacked thirds: root + 3rd + 5th.

| Triad | Formula | Example (C) | Sound |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Major** | 1 3 5 | C E G | Happy, stable |
| **Minor** | 1 ♭3 5 | C E♭ G | Sad |
| **Diminished** | 1 ♭3 ♭5 | C E♭ G♭ | Tense, unstable |
| **Augmented** | 1 3 ♯5 | C E G♯ | Dreamy, suspended, eerie |

**7th chords** — add a 4th stacked third (the 7th):

| Chord | Formula | Example | Vibe |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Major 7th** (maj7) | 1 3 5 7 | Cmaj7 = C E G B | Lush, dreamy, jazzy |
| **Minor 7th** (min7) | 1 ♭3 5 ♭7 | Cm7 = C E♭ G B♭ | Smooth, mellow, soul |
| **Dominant 7th** (7) | 1 3 5 ♭7 | C7 = C E G B♭ | Bluesy, tense, wants to resolve |
| **Min7♭5 / half-dim** (ø7) | 1 ♭3 ♭5 ♭7 | Cm7♭5 = C E♭ G♭ B♭ | Dark, jazzy ii in minor |
| **Diminished 7th** (°7) | 1 ♭3 ♭5 ♭♭7 | C°7 = C E♭ G♭ B♭♭ | Maximum tension, horror, pivot |

**Extensions** — keep stacking thirds past the 7th: **9th, 11th, 13th** (e.g. C9, Cm11, C13). Add richness/color; the 9/13 are jazz/neo-soul staples. → "lush Rhodes maj9 chords."
**sus chords** — replace the 3rd: **sus2** (1 2 5, e.g. Csus2 = C D G) open/airy; **sus4** (1 4 5, Csus4 = C F G) suspended, wants to resolve to the 3rd.
**add9** — add the 9th *without* the 7th (Cadd9 = C E G D) — bright, modern pop sparkle.
**Slash chords** — chord over a non-root bass: **C/G** (C with G bass), **C/E** (1st inversion) — smooth bass lines & pedal tones. → write descending bass with G–D/F♯–Em.

### Diatonic Chords & Roman Numerals

**Roman numeral analysis** — uppercase = major, lowercase = minor, ° = diminished, + = augmented. Numerals show *function*, key-independent.

**Major scale chords** (C major):
| I | ii | iii | IV | V | vi | vii° |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| C | Dm | Em | F | G | Am | B° |

**Natural minor chords** (A minor):
| i | ii° | III | iv | v | VI | VII |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Am | B° | C | Dm | Em | F | G |

→ In minor, raise the 7th (harmonic minor) to turn **v→V** and **VII→vii°** for a stronger pull home (Am: E major, G♯°).

### Harmonic Function

**Function** — each chord's role in the tension cycle:
- **Tonic (T)** = home, rest: **I, vi, iii**.
- **Subdominant / Predominant (PD)** = motion away, sets up tension: **IV, ii**.
- **Dominant (D)** = max tension, demands resolution (contains the tritone/leading tone): **V, vii°**.

**The cycle: T → PD → D → T** (e.g. I → IV → V → I). Tension builds then resolves; the engine of nearly all tonal music. → "Resolution" = dissonance moving to consonance; leading tone (7th degree) pulls up to tonic.

### Cadences (musical punctuation)

| Cadence | Motion | Sound / Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Perfect Authentic (PAC)** | V→I, both root position, melody ends on tonic | Strongest "full stop." End of section. |
| **Imperfect Authentic (IAC)** | V→I but inverted or melody on 3/5 | Softer resolution, comma. |
| **Plagal** | IV→I | "Amen" cadence; gentle, churchy. |
| **Half** | ends on V | Question, unresolved; mid-phrase pause. |
| **Deceptive** | V→vi (instead of I) | Surprise, "fake-out"; extends the phrase emotionally. |

### Voice Leading

**Voice leading** — how individual notes move from chord to chord; smooth = good.
- **Move the least** — keep **common tones** held, step other voices to nearest chord tone. (C→Am: share C & E, move G→A.)
- **Resolve tendency tones** — leading tone → tonic, 7th of a 7th chord → down a step.
- **Avoid parallel 5ths/octaves** (conceptually) — two voices moving in parallel P5/P8 thins the texture/sounds "empty"; use contrary or oblique motion instead.
- **Contrary motion** (voices move opposite directions) sounds strong and independent.

### Color & Advanced Harmony

**Modal interchange / borrowed chords** — pull a chord from the parallel mode for color:
- **iv in major** (minor 4-chord) — C major borrowing Fm → aching, "the saddest chord." → *Radiohead, Beatles* move.
- **♭VII** in major (B♭ in C) — rock/Mixolydian flavor.
- **♭VI & ♭III** — borrowed from parallel minor, epic/cinematic lift.
- **Picardy third** — end a minor piece on a *major* tonic (Am piece → A major chord); bright, hopeful surprise.

**Secondary dominants** — a dominant 7th of a chord other than I: **V/V** (D7 in C "of G"), **V/vi**, etc. Temporarily tonicizes the target, adds forward pull. → C: D7→G7→C ("five of five" turnaround).
**Tritone substitution** — replace a dom7 with the dom7 a tritone away (G7 → D♭7 before C); shares the tritone, gives a chromatic descending bass. Jazz staple.
**Pedal point** — sustain one note (usually tonic or dominant) in the bass while chords change above; builds tension/drone. → "droning tonic pedal under shifting chords."
**Suspension** — hold a note from the previous chord into a dissonance, then resolve down by step (4–3, 7–6, 9–8); creates a sigh/tension-release.

### Why Keys Feel Different (compact recap)

| Cause | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Pitch height** | Higher keys (B, C♯) sound brighter/tenser; lower (F, E♭) warmer/darker. |
| **Open strings / resonance** | D, G, A, E ring on guitar/violin → bright, "alive." E♭/A♭ favor brass/winds → mellow. |
| **Historical tuning** (well-temperament) | Pre-equal-temperament, each key had a unique color; tradition persists in our associations. |
| **Convention/association** | D = triumphant, C = pure, E♭ = heroic, B♭ = warm band key, F minor = mournful, C minor = stormy. |

→ On modern equal temperament keys are *technically* identical in interval ratios — the felt difference is pitch height, instrument resonance, and centuries of cultural association. For Suno, key choice matters less than mode/tempo/instrumentation; specify those instead.

## 🥁 Rhythm, Meter, Form & Arrangement

### Note Values & Rests

**Note value** — how long a note sounds, relative to the beat. Each value halves the one above it.

| Note | US name | UK name | Beats (in 4/4) | Rest looks like |
|------|---------|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| 𝅝 | Whole | Semibreve | 4 | hangs under line |
| 𝅗𝅥 | Half | Minim | 2 | sits on line |
| ♩ | Quarter | Crotchet | 1 | squiggle |
| ♪ | Eighth | Quaver | ½ | flag |
| ♬ | 16th | Semiquaver | ¼ | 2 flags |
| — | 32nd | Demisemiquaver | ⅛ | 3 flags |

**Dotted note** — adds half its own value. Dotted quarter = 1 + ½ = 1½ beats. Dot again (double-dot) = adds another quarter of original.
**Tie** — joins two notes of the same pitch into one sustained duration across a beat or barline (♩‿♩ = held 2 beats). Differs from a slur (slur = phrasing across *different* pitches).
**Tuplet** — squeeze X notes into the time of Y. **Triplet** = 3 in the space of 2 (eighth-note triplet = 3 per beat). Quintuplet = 5-in-4, etc.
→ Suno: `[triplet feel]`, `swung 16ths`, `dotted-eighth delay guitar`.

### Core Rhythm Terms

**Beat** — the steady pulse you tap your foot to. **Tempo** = how fast beats come.
**Measure / bar** — one group of beats bounded by barlines; the time signature sets how many.
**Downbeat** — beat 1, the strong landing of the bar. **Upbeat** — the last beat leading into the next downbeat (also the "&" lift before 1).
**Backbeat** — accent on beats **2 and 4** (the snare in rock/pop/funk). The engine of "groove."
**Anacrusis / pickup** — note(s) before the first downbeat ("Hap-py" before "BIRTH-day").
**Accent** — a stressed note, louder or harder than its neighbors. Placement = the soul of rhythm.

### Meter Types

**Meter** — how beats group into repeating patterns. Top number of the time sig = beats per bar; bottom = which note value gets the beat.

| Type | Definition | Examples | Feel |
|------|-----------|----------|------|
| **Simple** | Beat divides into **2** | 2/4, 3/4, 4/4 | Straight, square |
| **Compound** | Beat divides into **3** | 6/8, 9/8, 12/8 | Rolling, lilting, "galloping" |
| **Complex / asymmetric** | Beats grouped **unevenly** | 5/4, 7/8, 11/8 | Limping, off-kilter, propulsive |

**Reading compound**: 6/8 = 6 eighths, but felt as **2 big beats** of 3 (ONE-and-a TWO-and-a). Don't count all 6 evenly.

#### Odd / Asymmetric Meters

| Sig | Common grouping | Famous example |
|-----|-----------------|----------------|
| **5/4** | 3+2 or 2+3 | "Take Five" (Brubeck); *Mission: Impossible* |
| **7/8** | 2+2+3 | "Money" (Pink Floyd, 7/4); much Balkan & prog |
| **9/8** | 2+2+2+3 | "Blue Rondo à la Turk" (Brubeck) |
| **11/8** | 3+3+3+2 etc. | Prog metal; Bulgarian dance |

**Mixed / changing meter** — time sig shifts bar-to-bar (e.g., 4/4 → 7/8 → 4/4). Prog, math rock, film scores. Notated with a new sig at each change.
**Additive meter** — long bar built from uneven cells. **Balkan 2+2+3** ("aksak"/"limping") = a 7 felt as short-short-LONG. Write the grouping over the sig.
→ Suno: `7/8 time`, `odd meter`, `5/4 groove`, `Balkan rhythm`, `polymetric`. Models are unreliable here — reinforce with tempo + genre tags.

### Feel & Subdivision

**Straight** — subdivisions evenly spaced (8th notes split the beat 50/50). Rock, pop, EDM.
**Swing / shuffle** — first of each pair is longer. The "long-short" lope of jazz, blues, shuffle.
**Swing ratio** — long:short. **2:1** = triplet swing ("hard"). ~**1.5:1** = lighter swing. 1:1 = straight. Jazz "swing" floats and tightens with tempo.

| Feel | Ratio | Genre |
|------|-------|-------|
| Straight | 1:1 | Rock, EDM, pop |
| Light swing | ~1.5:1 | Pop-jazz, neo-soul |
| Hard swing / shuffle | 2:1 (triplet) | Blues, swing, boogie |
| Dotted (extreme) | 3:1 | Some hip-hop, trap hats |

**Pocket** — the deep, locked feel when drums + bass sit perfectly together. The "in-the-pocket" sweet spot.
**Laid-back** — playing a hair *behind* the beat (relaxed, soulful, J Dilla, reggae). **Pushed / on-top** — a hair *ahead* (urgent, punk, dance).
**Groove** — the cumulative rhythmic feel created by *consistent* placement, accents, and micro-timing. Repetition + slight tension = groove.
→ Suno: `swung 16ths`, `shuffle groove`, `laid-back pocket`, `J Dilla drunk drums`, `straight four-on-the-floor`.

### Syncopation & Cross-Rhythm

**Syncopation** — accenting **weak** beats or off-beats (the "ands"), going *against* the expected strong-beat pulse. The core of funk, ska, reggae, Latin.
**The "and" counts** — subdivide a 4/4 bar: "**1** & **2** & **3** & **4** &". Off-beats = the "&"s. Hitting them = syncopation.
**Anticipation** — playing a note *just before* its expected beat (e.g., the chord lands on the "& of 4" instead of beat 1). Pushes energy forward.
**Polyrhythm** — two conflicting subdivisions at once. **3:2 (hemiola/cross-rhythm)** = 3 evenly-spaced notes against 2. Mnemonic: "*nice cup of tea*" (3 over 2). Foundational in African & Latin music.
**Polymeter** — two different *meters* running simultaneously (e.g., a 4-beat riff over a 3-beat cycle); they realign after their LCM (here, 12 beats).
**Hemiola** — temporarily regrouping (e.g., two bars of 3/4 played as three groups of 2). Common in Baroque cadences, waltzes, Latin (the 6/8↔3/4 shift).
→ Suno: `syncopated groove`, `3 against 2 polyrhythm`, `cross-rhythm`, `hemiola`.

### Tempo

**BPM (beats per minute)** — the speed of the pulse. Higher = faster.

| BPM | Feel | Typical genres |
|-----|------|----------------|
| 40–60 | Funereal, vast, meditative | Ambient, doom, ballad |
| 60–76 | Slow, heavy, intimate | Soul ballad, hip-hop (half-time) |
| 76–108 | Walking, relaxed groove | Hip-hop, reggae, downtempo |
| 108–120 | Mid, "head-nod," pop default | Pop, rock, house-adjacent |
| 120–128 | Driving dance pulse | House, disco, dance-pop |
| 128–140 | Energetic, peak-club | Trance, techno, big-room |
| 140–160 | Fast, intense | Dubstep (half-time feel), DnB-lite |
| 160–180 | Frantic | Drum & bass, footwork |
| 180+ | Extreme | Speedcore, fast punk, blast-beats |

#### Italian Tempo Terms

| Term | BPM (approx) | Meaning |
|------|--------------|---------|
| **Grave** | 20–40 | Very slow, solemn |
| **Largo** | 40–60 | Broad, slow |
| **Adagio** | 66–76 | Slow, at ease |
| **Andante** | 76–108 | "Walking pace" |
| **Moderato** | 108–120 | Moderate |
| **Allegro** | 120–156 | Fast, bright |
| **Vivace** | 156–176 | Lively |
| **Presto** | 168–200 | Very fast |
| **Prestissimo** | 200+ | As fast as possible |

**Rubato** — expressive "stolen time"; pushing/pulling tempo for feel (rejoins the pulse later). Common in solo piano, vocal ballads.
**Accelerando (accel.)** — gradually speeding up. **Ritardando / rallentando (rit.)** — gradually slowing.
**Fermata** (𝄐) — hold a note/rest longer than written, at the player's discretion. The "pause."
→ Suno: set BPM explicitly (`128 BPM`); `accelerando outro`, `rubato piano intro`, `ritardando ending`.

**Tempo × time signature**: tempo sets the *speed of one beat*; the time signature sets *how many beats per bar* and *what they group into*. Same 120 BPM feels totally different in 4/4 (square) vs 6/8 (rolling) vs 7/8 (lurching). A "fast" 6/8 at 160 can feel slower than a "slow" 4/4 at 90 because of how the beats subdivide.

### Dynamics

**Dynamics** — relative loudness. The single most under-used tool for emotion in modern productions.

| Mark | Name | Level |
|------|------|-------|
| **pp** | pianissimo | Very soft |
| **p** | piano | Soft |
| **mp** | mezzo-piano | Medium-soft |
| **mf** | mezzo-forte | Medium-loud |
| **f** | forte | Loud |
| **ff** | fortissimo | Very loud |
| **sfz** | sforzando | Sudden hard accent |

**Crescendo** (`<`) — gradually louder. **Decrescendo / diminuendo** (`>`) — gradually softer.
**Role in build/release**: dynamics *are* the energy arc. A quiet verse → swelling pre-chorus → full chorus is a dynamic crescendo mapped onto song form. Drop the dynamics (subtract layers) right before a big moment so the moment hits harder.
→ Suno: `dynamic build`, `whispered verse, explosive chorus`, `crescendo into drop`, `quiet bridge`.

### Articulations

| Articulation | Symbol | Effect |
|--------------|--------|--------|
| **Staccato** | dot above note | Short, detached, clipped |
| **Legato** | slur / "smooth" | Connected, flowing, no gaps |
| **Accent** | `>` | Emphasized attack |
| **Marcato** | `^` | Marked, forceful (stronger than accent) |
| **Tenuto** | `–` over note | Held full value, slight stress |
| **Slur** | curved line over pitches | Play smoothly as one phrase (no re-articulation) |

→ Suno: `staccato strings`, `legato pad`, `marcato brass stabs`, `punchy detached bass`.

### Song Structure — Sections & Their Jobs

| Section | Job in the song |
|---------|-----------------|
| **Intro** | Set key, tempo, mood; pull the listener in. Often a stripped hook. |
| **Verse** | Tell the story; advance lyrics; lower energy than chorus. Melody usually repeats, words change. |
| **Pre-chorus** | Lift/transition; build tension that resolves into the chorus. |
| **Chorus** | The emotional + melodic peak; the repeated payoff; the "message." Highest energy, most layers. |
| **Post-chorus** | A secondary hook *after* the chorus (often a riff, "oh-oh" vocal, or beat moment) to extend the high. |
| **Hook** | The single catchiest fragment — melodic/lyrical/rhythmic — designed to lodge in memory. Can live anywhere. |
| **Refrain** | A recurring line (often the title) repeated at a section's end, without a full separate chorus. |
| **Bridge / Middle-8** | Contrast section (new chords/melody/lyric angle); breaks repetition before the final chorus. |
| **Breakdown** | Strip back to a sparse element (often drums/bass or a cappella) to reset energy. |
| **Drop** | EDM payoff — the full beat/bass slams in after a build; the "chorus" of dance music. |
| **Build / Riser** | Rising tension (filter sweep, snare roll, riser FX) that sets up a drop or chorus. |
| **Vamp** | A repeated short progression looped for groove, often under ad-libs or to close out. |
| **Turnaround** | A short chord move at a section's end that loops you back to the top (esp. blues/jazz). |
| **Outro / Coda** | The ending; wind down, fade, or final statement. |
| **Interlude** | A short instrumental/atmospheric passage between sections. |
| **Solo** | Featured instrumental showcase over the form (guitar/sax/synth). |
| **Ad-lib** | Improvised vocal flourishes (riffs, "yeah," echoes) layered over a section. |

### Song Forms

| Form | Structure | Genres |
|------|-----------|--------|
| **Verse–Chorus** | V–C–V–C–Br–C | Pop, rock, country (modern default) |
| **AABA (32-bar)** | A–A–B–A (8 bars each) | Jazz standards, Tin Pan Alley, early pop |
| **12-bar blues** | I–I–I–I–IV–IV–I–I–V–IV–I–V | Blues, rock'n'roll, R&B |
| **Strophic (AAA)** | Same music, new verses | Folk, ballads, hymns, "Blowin' in the Wind" |
| **Through-composed** | No repeats; continuous new material | Art song, prog, classical, "Bohemian Rhapsody" |
| **Verse–Chorus–Bridge** | Int–V–PC–C–V–PC–C–Br–C–Out | Modern pop standard |
| **Sonata-lite** | Exposition–Development–Recap | Classical; some prog/cinematic |
| **EDM build–drop** | Intro–Build–Drop–Breakdown–Build–Drop–Outro | House, dubstep, trance, festival EDM |

### Arrangement Principles

**Tension & release** — the master principle. Build expectation (rising pitch, density, volume, harmonic instability), then resolve it (the drop, the chorus, the "home" chord). Music is the management of expectation.
**Contrast** — sections must *differ* (energy, density, register, rhythm) or the ear tunes out. Verse low/sparse → chorus high/full.
**Layering & subtraction** — add elements to grow energy; **remove** them to create space and set up a hit. The pre-chorus drop-out before a chorus = subtraction.
**Frequency space** — give each part its own band so the mix stays clear: sub/kick (lows), bass + low synths (low-mids), vocals/guitars/keys (mids), cymbals/air/synths (highs). Don't stack instruments in the same register.
**Energy map** — chart the song's intensity over time as a rising line with dips before peaks. Each chorus should generally feel *bigger* than the last (final chorus = climax: add octave vocal, harmonies, key change, extra percussion).
**Motif / theme-and-variation** — a short memorable idea (motif) repeated and transformed (re-harmonized, re-orchestrated, rhythmically displaced) to give unity + development.
**Transitions** — the glue between sections:

| Device | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Drum fill** | Signals a section change; propels the turn |
| **Riser / sweep** | Builds tension into a drop/chorus |
| **Reverse cymbal / FX swell** | Smooth lift into a downbeat |
| **Drum drop-out / silence** | Sudden gap → the next hit lands huge |
| **Filter sweep** | Open up (energy in) or close down (energy out) |
| **Impact / boom** | Marks the downbeat of a major section |

### Energy Arc — 3.5-min Pop Song (example)

| Time | Section | Energy | What's happening |
|------|---------|--------|------------------|
| 0:00 | **Intro** | ▂ | Hook motif on one instrument; set mood |
| 0:12 | **Verse 1** | ▃ | Vocal + minimal beat; story begins |
| 0:38 | **Pre-chorus** | ▅ | Add layers, rising melody, build tension |

→ Suno: structure with meta-tags — `[Intro]`, `[Verse]`, `[Pre-Chorus]`, `[Chorus]`, `[Post-Chorus]`, `[Bridge]`, `[Final Chorus]`, `[Outro]`. Use `[Build]` and `[Drop]` for EDM; add `[Instrumental]` or `[Guitar Solo]` for solos.

## 🎚️ Melody, Timbre, Production & Genre Cheat-Codes

### Melody — The Tune

**Motif** — smallest memorable musical idea (2–7 notes); the DNA of a melody. Beethoven's 5th = 4-note motif.
**Phrase** — a complete musical "sentence," usually 2 or 4 bars; breathes like a spoken line.
**Period** — two phrases in a question→answer pair (antecedent + consequent).
**Melodic contour** — the shape of pitch over time. Audiences remember contour before exact notes.

| Contour | Shape | Feel |
|---|---|---|
| **Ascending** | pitch climbs | rising tension, lift, hope |
| **Descending** | pitch falls | release, resignation, calm |
| **Arch** | up then down | classic, satisfying, "complete" |
| **Wave/undulating** | up-down-up | conversational, natural, vocal |
| **Static/repeated** | flat | chant, hypnotic, urgent (rap, drone) |

**Range** — distance from lowest to highest note. Pop hooks live in ~1 octave; ballads stretch wider for drama.
**Tessitura** — where the melody *sits* most of the time (its comfort zone), distinct from absolute range. High tessitura = strain/intensity.
**Conjunct (steps)** — moving by 2nds; smooth, singable, "stepwise." Most pop verses.
**Disjunct (leaps)** — 3rds+; dramatic, harder to sing. A big leap = an emotional spike ("Some-WHERE over the rainbow," octave leap).
→ Mix mostly steps with one signature leap for a memorable, singable hook.

**Chord tones** — notes inside the underlying chord; stable, restful landing points.
**Passing tone** — non-chord note bridging two chord tones stepwise (off-beat, transient).
**Neighbor tone** — steps away from a chord tone and back (upper/lower).
**Tension tones** — 7ths, 9ths, suspensions, blue notes; pull toward resolution. Color and ache.
**Melodic rhythm** — the rhythm *of the tune* independent of pitch; often the true identity of a hook (clap the rhythm of "Happy Birthday" — still recognizable).

**Repetition & variation** — repeat to lodge it; vary to keep it alive. Rule of thumb: state → restate → depart.
**Sequence** — repeat a motif at a higher/lower pitch level ("Penny Lane" style climbs). Free momentum.
**Call-and-response** — phrase A "asks," phrase B "answers"; vocal↔instrument or two vocals. Roots in gospel/blues/funk.

**The Hook** — the catchiest, most repeated fragment; the part you can't stop humming. Earns the song's title spot.

**Earworm factors (what makes melody catchy):**
- **Singability** — fits the human voice, mostly stepwise, comfortable range.
- **Rhythmic identity** — a distinctive, syncopated, or "off" rhythm prints harder than pitch.
- **Contour memorability** — a simple, strong shape (arch, signature leap).
- **Tension→resolution** — set up an expectation, then satisfy (or tease) it.
- **Repetition with a twist** — repeated enough to learn, varied enough not to bore.
- **The "gap fill"** — a leap up that the ear *expects* to be filled by descending steps; satisfying when paid off.
→ Suno: "infectious stepwise vocal hook, syncopated rhythm, octave leap on the title word, arch contour."

### Timbre & Instrumentation — The Color

**Timbre (tone color)** — *why* a trumpet and a violin playing the same pitch+volume sound different. Determined by harmonic overtone content + attack/transient + envelope. The "texture" of a sound.

| Family | Members | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Strings** | violin, viola, cello, bass; harp | warm, sustained, emotive; bowed=legato, pizzicato=plucked |
| **Brass** | trumpet, trombone, French horn, tuba | bold, brassy, heroic, can blare |
| **Woodwind** | flute, clarinet, oboe, sax, bassoon | breathy, reedy, lyrical, agile |
| **Keys** | piano, organ, Rhodes/Wurli, harpsichord | percussive+harmonic, full-range |
| **Guitars** | acoustic, electric (clean/crunch/lead), bass | rhythm or lead; defines rock/folk/funk |
| **Synths** | analog, FM, wavetable, sampler | anything — the chameleon |
| **Percussion** | drum kit, congas, timpani, mallets, electronic | groove, accent, drive |
| **Vocals** | lead, harmony, choir, spoken, vocoder | the most human, most central timbre |

**Synth basics:**
- **Oscillator** — generates the raw waveform (the pitch source).
- **Waveforms:** **Sine** = pure, round, sub-bass/whistle. **Triangle** = soft, hollow, flute-like. **Square/pulse** = hollow, buzzy, retro/chiptune, woody. **Saw** = bright, buzzy, rich (the workhorse — leads, basses, supersaws).
- **Filter** — sculpts harmonics. **Cutoff** = where it starts removing frequencies; **Resonance** = boost at the cutoff point (squelch/whistle). Low-pass = warm/dark; high-pass = thin/airy.
- **ADSR envelope** — shapes a parameter over a note's life: **A**ttack (fade-in), **D**ecay (drop to…), **S**ustain (held level), **R**elease (fade-out after key-up). Slow attack = pad swell; instant attack + short decay = pluck.
- **LFO** — slow oscillator that *modulates* another parameter (pitch=vibrato, filter=wobble, volume=tremolo). Dubstep wobble = LFO on filter cutoff.

| Synth role | Sound | ADSR feel |
|---|---|---|
| **Pad** | lush, sustained, background harmony | slow attack, long release |
| **Lead** | cutting, soloing, melodic | fast attack, sustained |
| **Pluck** | short, percussive, arpeggiated | fast attack, short decay, low sustain |
| **Bass** | low, foundational, often mono | fast attack, tight |

→ Suno: name the timbre, not just the instrument — "warm Rhodes," "gritty supersaw lead," "breathy sub-bass," "pizzicato strings."

### The Frequency Spectrum

| Band | Range | What lives here | Mixing note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sub-bass** | 20–60 Hz | kick fundamental, 808s, sub | *Feel* not hear; high-pass everything else to clear it; mono it |
| **Bass** | 60–250 Hz | bass guitar/synth, kick body, low toms | Power & warmth; muddy if cluttered — carve kick vs bass |
| **Low-mids** | 250–500 Hz | guitar/piano body, snare warmth, vocal weight | The "mud zone" — cut here to declutter a busy mix |
| **Mids** | 500 Hz–2 kHz | vocal core, guitar/snare presence, most melody | Where the ear is most sensitive; the heart of the mix |
| **High-mids** | 2–6 kHz | vocal intelligibility, attack/bite, snare crack | Boost for clarity; harsh/fatiguing if overdone |
| **Presence/highs** | 6–12 kHz | cymbals, sibilance, "sheen," consonants | Detail & definition; de-ess sibilance around 5–8 k |
| **Air** | 12–20 kHz | "sparkle," openness, breath | A gentle shelf boost = expensive, open sound |

→ Mixing maxim: **every instrument owns a frequency lane.** Cut where another sound needs to shine (complementary EQ).

### Production & Mixing Vocab

**EQ (equalization)** — boost/cut specific frequency bands. Subtractive (cut the bad) usually beats additive (boost the good).
**Compression** — narrows dynamic range; tames peaks, adds punch/glue. **Threshold** = level where it kicks in. **Ratio** = how hard it squashes (4:1 firm, ∞:1 limiting). **Attack** = how fast it clamps (slow attack lets transients through = punch). **Release** = how fast it lets go (sets the "pump").
**Sidechain / pumping** — duck one signal by another; classic = bass/pads ducked by the kick → the breathing "pump" of house/EDM. → "sidechained pumping pads."

**Reverb** — simulated space/ambience.

| Type | Character | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Room** | small, tight, natural | glue, realism |
| **Hall** | large, lush, long tail | epic, orchestral, ballad |
| **Plate** | bright, dense, smooth | vocals, snares (classic studio sound) |
| **Spring** | boingy, retro | surf guitar, dub, amps |

**Delay (echo):** **Slapback** — single fast echo (~80–120 ms); rockabilly/50s. **Ping-pong** — echoes bounce L↔R; width & motion. **Dotted (1/8 dotted)** — tempo-synced, weaves between the beat (the "U2/The Edge" guitar).
**Saturation family (grit):** **Saturation** (warmth/glue) → **Overdrive** (soft clip, bluesy) → **Distortion** (harder clip, rock) → **Fuzz** (woolly, broken) → **Bitcrush** (digital, lo-fi).
**Modulation FX:** **Chorus** = thicken/widen (detuned copies). **Flanger** = jet-whoosh sweep. **Phaser** = swirly hollow notches (funk guitar).
**Stereo & space:** **Panning** = L↔R placement. **Stereo width** = how wide (keep lows mono). **Automation** = parameter changes drawn over time.
**Signal flow & loudness:** **Bus/group** = channels processed together. **Headroom** = dB before clipping (~6 dB on master). **Gain staging** = healthy levels throughout. **Transient** = the sharp initial attack. **Limiter** = brick-wall ceiling. **LUFS** = perceived loudness (streaming ≈ −14). **Dry vs wet** = unprocessed vs effected.

### Vocal Production

**Double-tracking** — record/copy a vocal twice, pan L/R; instant thickness & width.
**Harmony stack** — layered 3rds/5ths/octaves; choir-like lushness.
**Ad-libs** — improvised asides/echoes panned around the lead (the "yeah!", "uh", responses).
**Autotune** — pitch correction. *Transparent* (natural) vs *hard "T-Pain"* (fast retune = robotic, glassy effect).
**De-esser** — frequency-specific compressor taming harsh "S/T" sibilance (~5–8 kHz).
**Reverb throw** — momentary reverb burst on one word/phrase-end for drama.
**Formant** — vocal-tract resonances defining vowel character & perceived size; shift them = chipmunk/monster without changing pitch.
→ Suno: "lush stacked harmonies, panned ad-libs, subtle autotune, intimate doubled lead vocal."

### Arrangement Density & Space

**The principle:** *contrast creates impact.* A drop hits because the build emptied first; a vocal soars because the arrangement parted for it.
- **Density** = how many elements sound at once. Sparse verse → dense chorus = built-in lift.
- **Space** = strategic silence/thinning. "What you leave out" defines the groove (funk, dub, minimal techno).
→ Suno: "sparse intimate verse building to a wall-of-sound chorus," "minimal arrangement, lots of space."

# PART II — POETRY

*The craft of words that move in time: how a line lands in the ear, and how meaning is built from sound, image, and shape.*

---

## 🪶 Poetic Sound — Meter, Rhyme & Sonic Devices

Meter is rhythm you can count. Scansion is how you read it off the page. Everything below is the toolkit for making lines *land* in the ear — which is exactly what a lyric has to do.

### Scansion — Reading the Beat

**Scansion** — marking which syllables are stressed (strong) vs unstressed (weak) to expose a line's rhythm.
**Stressed (ictus)** — the syllable the voice naturally punches. Mark `ˊ` or `/` or CAPS.
**Unstressed (slack)** — the throwaway syllable. Mark `˘` or `×`.
Example: `the CURfew TOLLS the KNELL of PARTing DAY` → `˘ ˊ ˘ ˊ ˘ ˊ ˘ ˊ ˘ ˊ` (iambic pentameter).

**Notation systems** —
| System | Stressed | Unstressed |
|---|---|---|
| Classical | `ˉ` (long/macron) | `˘` (breve) |
| Modern English | `ˊ` or `/` | `˘` or `×` |
| Plain text | CAPS | lowercase |
| Drummer-speak | DUM | da |

**Why scansion matters for singability** — a melody assigns long notes/downbeats to certain syllables. If the *word's* natural stress fights the *music's* stress, you get **mis-stress** ("be-CAUSE" sung "BE-cause"). Scan the line, scan the melody, line the stresses up.
→ Pop demo: say the lyric out loud like speech first. Wherever you naturally hit it hard is where the downbeat wants to be.

### Metrical Feet

A **foot** is one repeatable stress-unit. Disyllabic = 2 syllables, trisyllabic = 3.

| Foot | Pattern | Stress | Example | Feel / Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Iamb** | da-DUM | ˘ ˊ | *be-LIEVE*, *the SUN* | natural English heartbeat; default "talking" rhythm |
| **Trochee** | DUM-da | ˊ ˘ | *GAR-den*, *TI-ger* | falling, insistent, chant/nursery ("TY-ger, TY-ger") |
| **Spondee** | DUM-DUM | ˊ ˊ | *HEART-BREAK*, *DEAD WEIGHT* | two hammer-blows; weight, emphasis, a brake |
| **Pyrrhic** | da-da | ˘ ˘ | *in the*, *of a* | two slacks; speeds the line, fills between stresses |
| **Anapest** | da-da-DUM | ˘ ˘ ˊ | *un-der-STAND*, *in the NIGHT* | galloping, rising; limericks, patter, momentum |
| **Dactyl** | DUM-da-da | ˊ ˘ ˘ | *MER-ri-ly*, *EL-e-phant* | tumbling, falling; waltz feel, epic ("PIC-ture the") |
| **Amphibrach** | da-DUM-da | ˘ ˊ ˘ | *re-MEM-ber*, *ro-MAN-tic* | lilting, rolling; limerick lines, light verse |
| **Cretic / Amphimacer** | DUM-da-DUM | ˊ ˘ ˊ | *SAVE the QUEEN*, *AF-ter-NOON* | bracketed stresses; emphatic tag, slogans |
| **Bacchius** | da-DUM-DUM | ˘ ˊ ˊ | *the LONG ROAD* | rising into a double-hit; solemn, building |
| **Antibacchius** | DUM-DUM-da | ˊ ˊ ˘ | *SLOW DAN-ger* | double-hit then drop; rare, ponderous |
| **Molossus** | DUM-DUM-DUM | ˊ ˊ ˊ | *GO! DON'T! STOP!* | three pure stresses; max weight, hard to sustain |
| **Tribrach** | da-da-da | ˘ ˘ ˘ | *(in)-a-ble-to* | three slacks; pure speed, rare in English |

→ Songwriting: **anapests/dactyls = swing & gallop** (triplet feel, "Subterranean Homesick Blues"); **iambs = conversational verse**; **trochees/spondees = hooks & shouts** (front-loaded stress hits the downbeat hard).

### Line Lengths (feet per line)

| Name | Feet | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Monometer | 1 | rare, novelty |
| Dimeter | 2 | short, punchy |
| Trimeter | 3 | ballad "6" lines, hymns |
| **Tetrameter** | 4 | **the song line** — ballads, hymns, most pop verse |
| **Pentameter** | 5 | **the verse line** — Shakespeare, blank verse, sonnets |
| Hexameter | 6 | a.k.a. **Alexandrine** (iambic hex); stately, can drag |
| Heptameter | 7 | the **fourteener** (iambic hept); splits into 8+6 |
| Octameter | 8 | very long; "The Raven" (trochaic oct) |

→ Most singable English lyric lives in **tetrameter and trimeter**, alternating. That's not an accident — it's the ballad engine.

### Named Meters

**Iambic pentameter** — 5 iambs, 10 syllables. "shall I com-PARE thee TO a SUM-mer's DAY". English's prestige meter.
**Iambic tetrameter** — 4 iambs. "the WOODS are LOVE-ly DARK and DEEP". Tighter, songlike.
**Common / Ballad meter** — alternating iambic tetrameter & trimeter, **8.6.8.6**, rhyming ABAB or ABCB. "a-MAZ-ing GRACE how SWEET the SOUND / that SAVED a WRETCH like ME". The backbone of hymns, folk, and half of pop.
**Long meter** — 8.8.8.8 (all tetrameter). **Short meter** — 6.6.8.6.
**Fourteeners** — iambic heptameter (14 syllables); ballads are fourteeners broken in half.
**Blank verse** — unrhymed iambic pentameter. Metered, no rhyme.
**Free verse** — no fixed meter *and* no rhyme scheme. Rhythm by ear, line breaks by intent.
→ "Common meter" is why so many lyrics are interchangeable with hymn tunes — same 8.6.8.6 skeleton (try singing "Amazing Grace" words to the Gilligan's Island theme).

### Metrical Variation (the anti-robot rules)

Perfect, unbroken meter sounds **mechanical** — a metronome, not a voice. Variation keeps it human.

**Substitution** — swapping in a different foot:
- **Trochaic inversion** — a trochee where an iamb's expected, usually line-start. "**NEV-er** shall I forget."
- **Spondaic substitution** — a spondee for emphasis/brake. "and **NO BIRDS** sing."
- **Pyrrhic substitution** — a slack pair speeds a stretch.

**Catalexis** — dropping the final unstressed syllable so the line ends on a stress: "TY-ger TY-ger BURN-ing BRIGHT."
**Acephalous / headless** — dropping the *first* unstressed syllable; the line opens on a stress.
**Promotion / demotion** — context bends a normally-weak syllable strong (or vice versa).
**Elision** — squashing syllables to fit: *o'er*, *heav'n*, *th'*.
**Line endings:** **Masculine** = ends on a stress (strong/rising). **Feminine** = ends with an extra unstressed syllable (weak/falling; "the SHAD-ows FALL-ing").
→ Lyric translation: a **feminine ending = the dangling "-ing"/"-er" that wants a pickup note**. Songwriters break meter exactly where the melody breathes.

### Rhyme Types

| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Perfect / full** | identical stressed vowel + all following sounds; different onset | *cat / hat*, *station / nation* |
| **Slant / near / half** | close but inexact (vowel or consonant off) | *worm / swarm*, *shape / keep* |
| **Eye rhyme** | looks like a rhyme, sounds different | *love / move*, *cough / bough* |
| **Identical / rime riche** | same sound *including* onset | *bare / bear*, *sea / see* |
| **Assonant rhyme** | shared vowel, different consonants | *lake / fate*, *blue / tooth* |
| **Consonant rhyme** | shared consonant frame, different vowel | *bell / ball*, *milk / walk* |
| **Mosaic / broken** | one word rhymed against several | *poet / know it*, *visit / is it* |
| **Masculine rhyme** | rhyme on a final stressed syllable | *re-LATE / cre-ATE* |
| **Feminine rhyme** | rhyme across 2 syllables, last unstressed | *MO-tion / O-cean* |
| **Triple (dactylic)** | rhyme across 3 syllables | *COM-fort-a-ble / un-COM-for-ta-ble* |
| **Internal** | rhyme *within* a line | "I BRING fresh SHOW-ers for the THIRST-ing FLOW-ers" |
| **End rhyme** | rhyme at line ends | the default |
| **Beginning / head rhyme** | rhyme/alliteration at line *starts* | "**BORN** to lose / **WORN** and used" |
| **Holorhyme** | entire lines rhyme | "*in EYE-land / I LAND*" |
| **Wrenched rhyme** | stress forced onto a weak syllable to rhyme | *lady / -ee* (folk ballads) |
| **Light / unstressed rhyme** | rhyme falls on an unstressed syllable | *sing-ING / fly-ING* |

→ Rap & modern pop live on **slant + assonant + multisyllabic mosaic** rhyme — *perfect* rhyme alone sounds nursery-rhyme. Assonance lets you rhyme almost anything ("*Picasso / lasso / fiasco*").

### Rhyme Schemes & Notation

Label each line's end-sound with a letter; same letter = they rhyme.

| Scheme | Pattern | Name / Use |
|---|---|---|
| **ABAB** | alternating | cross rhyme; ballad, sonnet quatrains |
| **AABB** | paired | couplets; punchy, closed, comic |
| **ABBA** | nested | **envelope / enclosed** rhyme |
| **ABCB** | 2nd & 4th only | loose **ballad** scheme (folk/pop standard) |
| **AAAA** | all same | **monorhyme**; insistent, hypnotic |
| **ABA BCB CDC** | interlocking | **terza rima** (Dante) |
| **ABABBCBC…** | carried-over | **chain rhyme**; links stanzas |

→ Most pop choruses are **ABAB or ABCB quatrains**; verses often loosen to ABCB or near-rhyme so the chorus's tighter rhyme *pops* by contrast.

### Sound Devices

**Alliteration** — repeated initial consonants. "**w**ild and **w**indy **w**ater." Front-loaded, grabby — great for hooks.
**Assonance** — repeated vowel sounds. "the r**ai**n in Sp**ai**n." Inner glue; smooths a line.
**Consonance** — repeated consonants anywhere (esp. ends). "pi**tt**er pa**tt**er."
**Sibilance** — repeated *s/sh/z* hiss. "**s**oft **c**ea**s**eless **s**inging." Whispery, soothing or sinister.
**Onomatopoeia** — word imitates sound. *buzz, crash, hush, thrum, clang.*
**Plosives** — hard-stop consonants *p/b/t/d/k/g*. Percussive, spat-out — anger, drive ("**b**ust a **b**eat").
**Liquids** — *l/r* (+ nasals *m/n*). Flowing, smooth, singable.
**Euphony** — pleasing, smooth sound (liquids, long vowels). "*lull / mellow / lagoon.*"
**Cacophony** — harsh, clashing sound (plosives, clustered consonants). "*grind / cracked / blunt thwack.*"
→ The *texture* of a line is consonant choice: **plosives punch the beat, liquids/sibilants let it glide.**

### Sonic Repetition (the "shape" devices)

**Refrain** — a line/section that recurs (the chorus, the hook). Anchors memory.
**Anaphora** — same word(s) at the *start* of successive lines. "**Every** breath… / **Every** move…" Builds, accumulates.
**Epistrophe** — same word(s) at the *end* of successive lines. "…of the people, …by the people, …for the people."
**Symploce** — anaphora + epistrophe together.
**Anadiplosis** — end of one line becomes start of the next. "fear leads to anger; **anger** leads to hate."
**Epizeuxis** — immediate word repeat. "*never never never.*"
→ Hooks are usually **anaphora or epistrophe + a refrain** — repetition is the cheapest, strongest mnemonic in music.

### Lineation & Flow

**End-stopped** — line ends with a grammatical pause; break and breath agree. "I wandered lonely as a cloud."
**Enjambment** — sense runs *over* the line break with no pause. "…I have eaten / the plums…" Creates pull, surprise, momentum.
**Caesura** — a strong pause *inside* a line (marked `‖`). "To err is human; ‖ to forgive, divine."
**Line break as a tool** — the last word of a line gets weight; short lines = fast/tense; the next line can spring a small surprise.
**White space / breath** — silence on the page = a rest in the music.
→ Lyrically: **end-stopped lines sit inside the bar**; **enjambment pushes across the barline** for forward drive. Caesura is your mid-bar rest or the spot an ad-lib slots in.

### Meter → Musical Rhythm (the seed)

**Core mapping:** **stressed syllables want strong beats** (1 and 3); unstressed want offbeats. An iamb (da-DUM) is a **pickup-into-downbeat**; a trochee (DUM-da) lands *on* the beat. **Anacrusis** = the unstressed pickup = the first slack of an iambic line. **Triple meters** (anapest/dactyl) map to **6/8, 12/8, swing**. A **feminine ending** = an extra weak syllable = a pickup into the next phrase. Perfect 1:1 alignment is square; great vocal rhythm *plays against* the meter. (Part III drills this fully.)

## 📜 Forms, Figurative Language & Rhetoric

### 🔒 Fixed & Closed Forms

| Form | Structure spec (lines · meter · rhyme) | Essence / use |
|---|---|---|
| **Sonnet (Shakespearean)** | 14 lines · iambic pentameter · ABAB CDCD EFEF GG | 3 quatrains build an argument, couplet snaps it shut; **volta** ~line 9 or at the couplet |
| **Sonnet (Petrarchan)** | 14 · iambic pent · octave ABBAABBA + sestet CDECDE/CDCDCD | Octave poses problem, sestet answers; **volta** at line 9 |
| **Sonnet (Spenserian)** | 14 · iambic pent · ABAB BCBC CDCD EE | Interlocking rhyme chains quatrains into one flow |
| **Villanelle** | 19 (5 tercets + quatrain) · ABA · 2 refrains alternate, both close the end | Obsessive, circling grief/fixation ("Do not go gentle") |
| **Sestina** | 39 (6 sixains + 3-line envoi) · no rhyme · 6 end-words rotate by fixed spiral (615243) | Hypnotic recurrence without rhyme; word-as-anchor |
| **Pantoum** | quatrains · lines 2&4 become lines 1&3 of the next; final loops to opening | Echoing, dreamlike forward-backward drift |
| **Ghazal** | 5+ autonomous couplets · **radif** (refrain) ends couplet 1 then line 2 of each · poet's name in final couplet | Longing, the divine, the beloved; each couplet a stand-alone jewel |
| **Haiku** | 3 lines · 5-7-5 (approx) · **kigo** (season word) + **kireji** (cutting/pivot) | Single perception, nature, the now |
| **Senryū** | 3 · 5-7-5 · no season word | Haiku shape, but human nature, wit, irony |
| **Tanka** | 5 · 5-7-5-7-7 | Haiku's image + a 2-line emotional turn |
| **Renga** | linked chain · alternating 5-7-5 / 7-7 by multiple poets | Collaborative; each link pivots off the last |
| **Limerick** | 5 · anapestic · AABBA (A's long, BB short) | Bawdy, comic, punchline on line 5 |
| **Ballad** | quatrains · **ballad meter** 8.6.8.6 · ABCB | Sung narrative, folk story; foundation of song verse |
| **Ode** | varies (Pindaric/Horatian/irregular) · often elevated | Sustained praise/address to a subject |
| **Elegy** | no fixed form · often solemn | Lament for the dead; mourning → consolation |
| **Blank verse** | unrhymed iambic pentameter | Speech-natural gravity; Shakespeare, Milton |
| **Free verse** | no meter, no rhyme, no fixed length | Rhythm from cadence/image/line-break alone |
| **Terza rima** | tercets · interlocking ABA BCB CDC… · closes with couplet | Forward-pulling chain; Dante's *Inferno* |
| **Ottava rima** | 8-line stanzas · iambic pent · ABABABCC | Narrative + couplet for wit/turn (Byron) |
| **Rondeau** | 15 · 2 rhymes · opening words return as refrain (lines 9 & 15) | Lyric circularity ("In Flanders Fields") |
| **Triolet** | 8 · ABaAabAB · lines 1,4,7 & 2,8 repeat | Tiny, music-box recurrence |
| **Cinquain (Crapsey)** | 5 · 2-4-6-8-2 syllables | Compact swell-and-collapse |
| **Acrostic** | varies · first letters of lines spell a word | Hidden vertical message |
| **Ekphrastic** | any form | Poem responding to a work of art |
| **Prose poem** | no line breaks; paragraph form | Poetic density in prose skin |
| **Concrete/shape** | typography forms an image | Visual shape = meaning |
| **Found poem** | reframes existing non-poetic text | Curation as authorship |
| **Golden shovel** | each line ends with successive words of a borrowed line (read down the right margin) | Homage embedded in the right edge |
| **Erasure/blackout** | redacts a source text to leave a poem | Subtraction as composition |
| **Aubade** | any form | Lovers parting at dawn; dawn as intruder |

→ **Songwriting:** the **ballad** (8.6.8.6 ABCB) is the genetic code of verse-chorus pop/folk; **villanelle/pantoum** refrain logic = the hook that recurs and accrues meaning each return.

### 🧱 Stanza Forms

| Stanza | Lines | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| **Couplet** | 2 | Aphorism, punchline, the "click" close (heroic couplet = rhymed iambic pent) |
| **Tercet** | 3 | Terza rima, haiku-shape, triadic balance |
| **Quatrain** | 4 | The workhorse — ballads, hymns, most song verses |
| **Quintain / Cinquain** | 5 | Limerick, the "extra" line that tips or turns |
| **Sestet** | 6 | Sonnet's answering half; sestina's sixain |
| **Septet** | 7 | Rhyme royal (ABABBCC) — narrative weight |
| **Octave** | 8 | Sonnet's problem-half; ottava rima |

→ Verse = quatrain, chorus = couplet-driven hook, bridge = a tercet/quintain that breaks the pattern.

### 🎭 Figurative Language

| Device | Definition | Tiny example |
|---|---|---|
| **Metaphor** | A is B; identity, no "like/as" | "Hope is the thing with feathers" |
| **Simile** | A *like/as* B; explicit comparison | "Cold as a January gravestone" |
| **Extended metaphor / conceit** | one metaphor sustained across a passage | Donne's lovers as compass legs |
| **Metonymy** | substitute an associated thing | "The **crown** ruled" (= the monarch) |
| **Synecdoche** | part stands for whole | "All **hands** on deck" |
| **Personification** | human traits to non-human | "The wind **complained**" |
| **Hyperbole** | deliberate overstatement | "I've told you a million times" |
| **Litotes / understatement** | affirm by negating the opposite | "Not bad" (= excellent) |
| **Verbal irony** | say opposite of meaning | "Lovely weather" in a storm |
| **Situational irony** | outcome defies expectation | A fire station burns down |
| **Dramatic irony** | audience knows what character doesn't | We see the killer; she doesn't |
| **Symbol** | concrete thing standing for an abstraction | A dove = peace |
| **Allegory** | sustained symbolic system | *Animal Farm* = revolution |
| **Oxymoron** | two contradictory words joined | "Bittersweet," "deafening silence" |
| **Paradox** | self-contradictory but true | "Less is more" |
| **Juxtaposition** | contrasting things side by side | Cradle beside a grave |
| **Pun / double-entendre** | wordplay on multiple meanings | "Time flies like an arrow" |
| **Allusion** | brief reference to known text/event | "He met his **Waterloo**" |
| **Apostrophe** | direct address to absent/abstract/dead | "O Death, where is thy sting?" |
| **Pathetic fallacy** | nature mirrors human emotion | Sky weeps at the funeral |
| **Synesthesia** | blend one sense into another | "A **loud** shirt," "**sweet** silence" |

→ Lyrics live on **concrete metaphor**; an **extended metaphor** across a whole song is the surest route to a memorable single ("Firework," "Iris").

### 👁️ Imagery — The Senses

**Visual** — sight. **Auditory** — sound. **Olfactory** — smell. **Gustatory** — taste. **Tactile** — touch/texture/temperature. **Kinesthetic** — movement ("the staggering crowd"). **Organic** — internal bodily sensation/emotion (hunger, dread, the gut-drop).

**Show, don't tell** — render the *evidence* of a feeling, not its label: not "she was sad" but "she left the porch light on for a year." **Concrete detail** beats abstraction. **Objective correlative** (Eliot) — an external object/situation that *evokes* a specific emotion without naming it.
→ The #1 lyric upgrade: trade an abstract noun ("pain," "freedom") for a sensory image that *makes* the listener feel it.

### 🏛️ Rhetorical & Structural Devices

| Device | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Anaphora** | repeat opening words | "**We shall** fight… **We shall** never surrender" |
| **Epistrophe** | repeat *ending* words | "…of the people, **by the people**, for the people" |
| **Chiasmus** | mirrored ABBA of ideas | "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" |
| **Antimetabole** | chiasmus with exact word reversal | "Ask not what your country can do for you—" |
| **Antithesis** | opposed ideas, parallel grammar | "the best of times… the worst of times" |
| **Asyndeton** | omit conjunctions | "I came, I saw, I conquered" |
| **Polysyndeton** | pile on conjunctions | "and… and… and…" |
| **Parallelism** | matched grammatical structure | "Like father, like son" |
| **Zeugma** | one word governs two, often differently | "He stole my heart **and** my wallet" |
| **Enumeratio / listing** | accumulate details into a catalog | Whitman's inventories |
| **Hypophora** | pose a question, then answer it | "What is life? A breath." |
| **Rhetorical question** | question asked for effect | "Isn't it ironic?" |
| **Volta / turn** | the pivot where argument/feeling shifts | Sonnet line 9; the bridge of a song |

→ **Anaphora** is the hook-builder ("**Every** breath you take, **every** move you make"); **the turn/volta** = the bridge that recontextualizes everything before it.

### 🌡️ Tone, Mood, Diction & Register

**Tone** — the *speaker's* attitude (ironic, tender, bitter). **Mood** — the *atmosphere the reader feels* (ominous, serene). Tone is the sender, mood is the received signal. **Diction** — word choice. **Register** — formality level (intimate / casual / formal / frozen). **High diction** — elevated, Latinate ("perish"). **Low diction** — colloquial, plain ("die"). **Denotation** = dictionary meaning; **Connotation** = emotional baggage ("home" vs "house").
→ Choosing **"ain't" vs "is not"** sets genre, character, and class in a single syllable — diction is the fastest worldbuilding a lyric has.

### 🎯 Poetic Strategy

**Concretion** — drive abstract → image; never state the emotion, build the object that produces it. **Point of view** — who speaks (I/you/we) and how close; *direct address* ("you") implicates the listener. **The turn** — every strong song pivots somewhere; locate it and earn it. **Economy** — every word load-bearing. **The killer last line** — end on the line that reframes or detonates. **Juxtaposition as engine** — meaning sparks in the *gap* between two unlike images.
→ Build the song from images, place the **turn** at the bridge, and spend your best line *last* — the final hook is what they hum walking away.

## 🎤 Lyric Writing, Flow & the Music↔Poetry Bridge

> Page poetry lives on the eye; lyric lives on the breath and the beat. Everything below bends poetic craft toward **singability, prosody, and prompts that actually steer an AI music tool.**

### 🪶 Lyric vs. Page Poetry — What Changes

**The melody does half the work** — the *tune* carries pitch and much of the rhythm, so the lyric can be plainer and still land. Lines that read flat often sing great; "poetic" lines often choke a melody.
**Repetition is a feature, not a flaw** — a chorus repeating a line four times is the *point*. Songs reward the loop.
**Rhyme density is higher & expected** — listeners can't re-scan a lyric, so rhyme + meter are the railings that keep them oriented.
**Open vowels for sustained/high notes** — you hold "**ah / oh / ay**" comfortably; you can't belt a high note on "**ick**." Put money words' stressed syllable on an open vowel.
**Consonant clusters fight the voice** — "stre**ngths**," "tw**elfths**" clog the mouth. Favor vowel-forward, flowing consonants (l, m, n, r, w, y).
**The line must breathe** — a lyric line = one phrase the singer can deliver in one breath.

| Dimension | Page Poetry | Song Lyric |
|---|---|---|
| Rhythm source | the words alone | words **+ melody + beat** |
| Repetition | suspect | structural, welcome |
| Rhyme | optional, subtle | denser, load-bearing |
| Vowels | semantic only | also *phonetic* (note-shape) |
| Line length | by eye/meter | by **breath** |
| Re-reading | allowed | impossible — clarity first |

### 🎚️ Prosody — Stress, Pitch & Breath (the Craft Sense)

> *Prosody* here = the **fit** between word and music. Bad prosody = the listener mishears the word or feels the line "fighting" the tune.

**Word-stress → musical stress** — put the syllable you'd naturally hit when *speaking* on the strong beat / higher note. "gui-**TAR**" (right), not "**GUI**-tar."
**Strong beats = stressed syllables** — beats 1 (and 3 in 4/4) are landing spots; money words and the rhyme arrive there.
**Vowel choice tracks pitch** — open **ah/oh/aw** for big, high, sustained notes; closed **ee/ih/oo** feel tight, intimate, fast.
**Line length = the singer's breath** — phrasing is governed by lungs, not margins. Want a breathless feel? Deny the breath.
**Rhyme placement** — land it on a stressed downbeat for power; push it to a weak beat for unease/playfulness.
**Singability test** — speak it at tempo, then hum it on one note. Jaw locks → a vowel's wrong; out of air → line's too long; word warps → stress is off.

### 🧱 Song Structure & Section Jobs

| Section | Job | Lyric texture |
|---|---|---|
| **Intro** | set mood, tease | minimal / instrumental / motif |
| **Verse** | *advance the story*, specific detail | most words, lowest repetition, evolves each pass |
| **Pre-Chorus** | lift & set up the drop | rising line, often one repeated phrase, builds tension |
| **Chorus** | the **title + universal emotional payoff** | simplest, most repeated, most memorable, *least* new info |
| **Post-Chorus** | extend the high / extra hook | a chant, "oh-oh," or tag |
| **Bridge** | contrast / twist / new angle | new melody + chords, the *turn*, fresh image — appears once |
| **Outro** | release / fade / final tag | repeat-out hook or stripped reprise |

**Verse vs. chorus** — Verse = *particular* (a name, a room, a Tuesday). Chorus = *universal* (what anyone feels). The verse earns the chorus; the chorus pays it off.
**What makes a great chorus** — singable on first hear, contains the title, simplest melody+lyric, emotionally widest, easy to belt. If only one line survives, it should be the chorus.
**Refrain** — a repeated *line* anchored inside verses (e.g., the last line of every verse). Cheaper than a full chorus, very sticky.
**Hook** — the single stickiest fragment (melodic, lyrical, or rhythmic) the song is *about*.

### 🪝 Hook Craft

**Three hook types** — **Melodic** (the tune you hum), **lyrical** (the phrase you quote — usually the title), **rhythmic** (the *cadence/groove*, e.g., a stuttered "we-we-will rock you").
**Title placement** — put the title where the music peaks: first line of chorus (statement), last line (payoff), or both.
**"The promise of the title"** — a great title raises a question or promises a feeling; the song delivers exactly that.
**Repetition thresholds** — a pop hook typically lands **3–5×** per song. Too few = forgettable; too many = annoying.
**Make it portable** — the best hooks survive out of context (chanted at a show). Short, vowel-forward, rhythmically distinct.

### 🔁 Rhyme & Flow in Modern Lyrics / Rap

**The bar** — one measure (usually 4 beats in 4/4). "**A 16**" = a 16-bar verse, the rap standard.
**Flow** — the *rhythm of your delivery* over the beat. Two rappers, same words, different flow = different song. Flow is melody for the percussive voice.
**The pocket** — the groove's sweet spot; your cadence locks with the drums so the bars feel *inevitable*.
**Cadence** — the repeating rhythmic shape of your lines; the signature of a flow.

| Flow device | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Straight / on-beat** | syllables on the quarter-notes — clear, classic |
| **Triplet flow** | 3 syllables per beat (the "Migos" gallop) |
| **Double-time** | twice the syllable rate without changing the beat — urgency |
| **Behind the beat** | drag slightly late — relaxed, cool, conversational |
| **Ahead / pushing** | land early — aggressive, anxious, driving |
| **Switching flows** | change cadence between sections to refresh the ear |

**Rhyme tools (beyond end-rhyme):**

| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Multisyllabic / compound** | rhyming whole clusters | *"area / scarier / barrier"* |
| **Internal rhyme** | rhymes *within* a line | *"my **mind**'s **inclined**…"* |
| **Assonance / vowel rhyme** | matching vowels, ignoring consonants | *"sk**y** / l**i**fe / f**i**re"* |
| **Slant / near rhyme** | close-but-not-exact — modern default | *"shape / weight," "home / bone"* |
| **Chain / linking rhyme** | end of one line rhymes into the *start* of the next | links bars into a run |

**Bars & breath** — end-stopped bars (rhyme + pause at the bar line) feel punchy; **spill-over / enjambment** (sense runs *past* the bar) creates momentum — the rap equivalent of run-on lines. Great MCs hide breaths in beat gaps.
**Content modes** — **Storytelling** (narrative arc), **braggadocio** (skill/status flex, punchline-dense), **conceptual** (one extended metaphor carrying the track).
**Punchlines & wordplay** — **Punchline** = setup then payoff zinger. **Entendre** = one phrase, two meanings. **Homophone play** = same sound, different word ("knight/night"). Reward the *re-listen*.

### 🎙️ Delivery

**Melodic vs. percussive** — melodic = pitched, sung-rap, hook-forward; percussive = voice as a drum, syllables as hits.
**Ad-libs** — background shouts/echoes in the gaps ("yeah," "uh," echoing the last word). Tag as *(ad-lib)* in lyrics.
**Phrasing vs. the beat** — *behind* = laid-back/soulful; *on* = tight/precise; *ahead* = urgent. Where you sit against the grid is a tone choice.

### ✍️ Writing Process Tips

**Write from the 7 senses** — sight, sound, smell, taste, touch, **plus** organic (gut/heartbeat) and kinesthetic (motion). "Don't say sad — show the cold coffee."
**Prosody-first** — sketch lyrics *against* the melody/groove early so stress and breath fit from the start.
**Title-first vs. hook-first** — a strong title generates the song's promise; or a melodic/rhythmic hook arrives and you write *toward* it. Both beat a blank page.
**Point of view & specificity** — pick a clear POV and stay concrete. **The universal through the specific**: name the diner, the song on the radio, the exact street.
**Kill cliché & filler** — "burning desire," "tears like rain," empty "yeah, baby, oh, just." If you've heard it, cut it or twist it.
**The rewrite** — first draft = get it down. Second pass = sharpen images, fix prosody, hunt the lazy rhyme, cut a syllable everywhere.

### 🌉 THE BRIDGE — Poetry ↔ Music Mapping

#### Poetic Foot / Meter ↔ Musical Feel

| Foot | Pattern | Feel | Musical home |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Iamb** (da-**DUM**) | weak-strong | conversational, forward | pickup/anacrusis into beat 1; most 4/4 verse |
| **Trochee** (**DUM**-da) | strong-weak | insistent, chant, on-the-beat | hooks, "**Hap**-py", downbeat starts |
| **Anapest** (da-da-**DUM**) | rising triple | galloping, rollicking | **6/8 & 12/8**, jigs, shuffles |
| **Dactyl** (**DUM**-da-da) | falling triple | waltzing, tumbling | **3/4 / 6/8 waltz**, triplet flows |
| **Spondee** (**DUM-DUM**) | strong-strong | heavy, emphatic | stabs, halftime hits, "**STOP. NOW.**" |
| **Pyrrhic** (da-da) | weak-weak | hurried connective | fast filler syllables between accents |

#### Key / Mode ↔ Lyric Tone

| Mode | Color | Lyric tone |
|---|---|---|
| **Major (Ionian)** | bright, resolved | affirmation, joy, love-won, anthems |
| **Minor (Aeolian)** | dark, settled | grief, longing, introspection, heartbreak |
| **Dorian** | minor + bright 6th | hopeful-melancholy, resilient sadness |
| **Phrygian** | minor + flat 2nd | menace, tension, Spanish/metal dread |
| **Mixolydian** | major + flat 7th | swagger, groove, bluesy confidence |
| **Lydian** | major + sharp 4th | wonder, dreaminess, cinematic awe |

#### BPM ↔ Syllable Density / Pacing

| Tempo | BPM | Lyric strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Ballad / slow** | 60–80 | long arching phrases, **melisma**, lots of space |
| **Mid / groove** | 90–115 | conversational, balanced words-per-bar, room for a story |
| **Up / pop-rock** | 116–132 | tighter phrasing, punchy lines, strong repeated hook |
| **Fast / dance / rap** | 133–160+ | clipped & percussive; *either* dense words-per-bar (rap) *or* sparse chant hooks (EDM) |

> Rule of thumb: **slower tempo buys syllable *length* (melisma); faster tempo buys syllable *count* (or forces sparsity).**

### 🤖 Prompt Engineering for AI Music (Suno)

#### Anatomy of a Strong Style Prompt

Stack these slots (comma-separated, most-important first; ~200 chars is plenty):

> **[genre/subgenre] + [mood] + [key/mode] + [tempo BPM + feel] + [instrumentation w/ timbre] + [production] + [vocal type & delivery] + [reference era]**

**Example skeleton:**
`neo-soul ballad, intimate and aching, D minor (Dorian lift), 72 BPM laid-back behind-the-beat, warm Rhodes, muted bass, brushed drums, vinyl warmth, breathy female alto, lo-fi 1970s`

**Slot tips** — name *timbre* not just instrument ("muted Rhodes"); specify *delivery* ("breathy," "belted," "spoken-word"); era/reference anchors the palette fast; **mode + BPM steer mood harder than adjectives.**

#### Meta-Tag Reference (Lyrics Box)

| Section tags | Energy / FX tags | Inline tags |
|---|---|---|
| `[Intro]` `[Verse]` `[Pre-Chorus]` | `[Build]` `[Drop]` `[Break]`/`[Breakdown]` | `(whispered)` `(belted)` |
| `[Chorus]` `[Post-Chorus]` `[Hook]` | `[Instrumental]` `[Guitar Solo]` | `(ad-lib: yeah)` |
| `[Bridge]` `[Refrain]` `[Outro]` | `[Beat Switch]` `[Tempo Up]` | `(harmonies)` `(call & response)` |

**Conventions** — one tag per line; keep names standard (Suno recognizes common section words best); inline parentheticals steer vocals/ad-libs. **Treat all tags as suggestions** — re-roll if ignored, and reinforce intent in the style box too.

#### Anti-"AI-ism" Tactics

Untended models drift to **AABB** nursery rhyme + 4/4 + obvious rhymes. Counter it:
- **Vary the rhyme scheme** — ABAB, slant, internal; avoid wall-to-wall AABB couplets.
- **Vary meter & line length** — uneven lines read human; metronomic lines read bot.
- **Specify mode & instrumentation** — "Dorian," "Lydian," "muted Rhodes" pull it out of generic-major-pop.
- **Request specific delivery** — "behind-the-beat," "spoken-word verse," "half-time bridge."
- **Control the hook** — give the exact hook line and where it repeats.
- **Kill clichés in your own lyrics** — the model amplifies whatever you feed it.

### 🧪 Worked Examples (Style Prompt + Tagged Lyrics)

**1. Neo-Soul Ballad**

> **Style:** `neo-soul ballad, aching and intimate, D Dorian, 72 BPM behind-the-beat, warm Rhodes + muted upright bass + brushed drums, vinyl warmth, breathy female alto, lush background harmonies, 1970s lo-fi`

```
[Verse]
Cold coffee on the windowsill, the radio's still on
You left your jacket on the chair like you'd be right back home
[Pre-Chorus]
And I keep the kettle warm (keep it warm)
[Chorus]
But you don't call, you don't call
I'm learning how the quiet sounds (how the quiet sounds)
You don't call at all
```
*Why it works:* concrete detail (cold coffee, jacket), open-vowel money words ("call," "all"), Dorian = resilient sadness, behind-the-beat = soulful drag, ad-lib echoes.

**2. Boom-Bap Rap (16 + hook)**

> **Style:** `90s boom-bap hip-hop, dusty and confident, E minor, 88 BPM in the pocket, chopped soul sample, thick upright-bass loop, crackling vinyl drums, raspy male baritone, conversational behind-the-beat flow, golden-era`

```
[Verse]
Pen hit the pad like a hammer to anvil (anvil)
Every bar that I scribble's a chapter, a mantra, a handle (yeah)
Internal rhymes runnin' rivers through the channel,
spill past the bar line, I never even ran low —
[Hook]
That's the pocket, that's the pocket where I live (uh)
Drop the needle, watch the whole room start to lift
```
*Why it works:* multisyllabic chains (anvil/mantra/handle/channel), enjambment ("spill past the bar line"), pocket as the hook concept, ad-libs doubling rhymes.

**3. Bardcore / Medieval Folk**

> **Style:** `bardcore medieval folk, wistful and wandering, A Aeolian into Dorian, 96 BPM 6/8 lilt, lute + hurdy-gurdy + wooden flute + frame drum, reverberant stone-hall, clear mixed-gender harmonies, no modern instruments`

```
[Verse]
By candle-low the rivers run, the watchman calls the hour
[Verse]
We trade our coin for crust and song beneath the broken tower
[Chorus]
Oh carry me, oh carry me, where the green roads bend
Oh carry me, oh carry me, to my kin again
```
*Why it works:* 6/8 anapestic lilt (gallop), open "oh/me/again" for sustained notes, refrain-style repeated chorus, archaic-concrete imagery, modal color.

**4. Epic Cinematic Build**

> **Style:** `epic cinematic trailer music, ominous to triumphant, C Lydian over D minor pedal, 60→120 BPM accelerating, low brass + taiko + choir + soaring strings, huge hall reverb, wordless choir then belted female lead, Zimmer-esque`

```
[Intro][Instrumental]
[Build]
A spark beneath the ash (beneath the ash)
[Pre-Chorus] (choir swells)
We were not made to kneel
[Drop][Chorus]
RISE — and the dark comes undone
RISE — we are the breaking sun
[Bridge] (half-time, strings only)
And if we fall, we fall as light
```
*Why it works:* Lydian = wonder/awe, tempo accel = build, spondaic shouts ("RISE—") on stabs, open belt vowels ("sun/undone"), half-time bridge for contrast.

### 📜 The 10 Commandments of Lyric Craft

1. **Serve the breath** — if you can't sing it in one breath at tempo, cut it.
2. **Stress lands on the strong beat** — never mis-stress a word to fit.
3. **Open vowels on the money notes** — save "ah/oh" for the peaks.
4. **The chorus is the simplest, widest, most-repeated thing** — and it holds the title.
5. **Universal through the specific** — name the diner, not "the pain."
6. **Earn repetition; don't pad with filler.**
7. **Rhyme is a tool, not a tyrant** — break AABB; use slant, internal, multisyllabic.
8. **Show senses, don't state feelings** — cold coffee over "I'm sad."
9. **Match mode & tempo to the emotion** — Dorian + 72 BPM says more than ten adjectives.
10. **Rewrite** — first drafts are clay; the song is in the second pass.

> **Prompt-craft corollary:** *Tell the AI the mode, the BPM+feel, the timbre, the delivery, and the exact hook — then re-roll without mercy.*

## Appendix C — 🃏 The One-Screen Cheat Card
*The 20% you'll use 80% of the time.*

**HARMONY** · Major = bright, minor = dark · Mode brightness (bright→dark): **Lyd · Ion · Mixo · Dor · Aeo · Phryg · Locr** · Function cycle **T → PD → D → T** (I → IV/ii → V → I) · The "4 chords": **I–V–vi–IV** · Sad version: **vi–IV–I–V** · Cadences: V→I = full stop, IV→I = "amen," ends-on-V = question, V→vi = fake-out · Borrow **iv in major** = the saddest chord · End minor on major (**Picardy 3rd**) = hope.

**RHYTHM** · Backbeat = snare on **2 & 4** · 4/4 = default, 6/8 = rolling, 3/4 = waltz, odd meters = lurch · Swing = long-short lope · BPM: 60–80 slow/intimate · 90–115 groove · 120–130 dance/pop · 140+ energy · Energy arc: each chorus bigger than the last; **subtract before the big hit.**

**SONG FORM** · `Intro → Verse → Pre-Chorus → Chorus → Verse → Pre-Chorus → Chorus → Bridge → Final Chorus → Outro` · Verse = specific/story, Chorus = universal/title/simplest, Bridge = the turn.

**METER** · iamb da-**DUM** (talk) · trochee **DUM**-da (chant/hook) · anapest da-da-**DUM** (gallop/6-8) · dactyl **DUM**-da-da (waltz) · spondee **DUM-DUM** (stabs) · **Ballad meter = 8.6.8.6, ABCB** = half of all songs.

**RHYME** · Don't rhyme everything AABB — use **slant, internal, multisyllabic, assonance** · Land rhyme on the **downbeat** for power · Stressed syllable on the strong beat, **always**.

**LYRIC** · Show senses, don't state feelings · **Universal through the specific** · Open vowels (ah/oh) on the big notes · One image > one adjective · Spend your best line **last**.

**PROMPT SKELETON** · `[genre] + [mood] + [key/mode] + [BPM + feel] + [instruments w/ timbre] + [production] + [vocal + delivery] + [era]` · Tags: `[Verse] [Pre-Chorus] [Chorus] [Bridge] [Build] [Drop] [Outro]` · **Mode + BPM beat ten adjectives.** Re-roll without mercy.

## Appendix D — 🏋️ Practice Drills
*Craft is a muscle. Ten reps, escalating.*

1. **Interval ear** — hum the first two notes of a song for each interval in the table; learn to recognize a P5 vs a m3 by feel.
2. **Reharmonize a nursery rhyme** — take "Twinkle Twinkle" and swap its I–IV–V for I–V–vi–IV, then for a minor i–VI–III–VII. Notice the mood flip.
3. **Scan a verse** — mark stresses on a verse of a song you love. Find where it *breaks* perfect meter — that's where it breathes.
4. **One image, no abstractions** — write 4 lines about heartbreak without the words sad, hurt, love, heart, or pain. Use only things you can see/touch/hear.
5. **Rhyme ladder** — take one word and build a multisyllabic slant-rhyme chain of 6 (e.g., *orange → door hinge → more strange…*). Stretch the assonance.
6. **Mode swap** — write the same 2-line hook melody, then prompt it once in Aeolian and once in Dorian. Hear the "resilient sadness" shift.
7. **The 8.6.8.6 challenge** — write a verse in strict ballad meter (8-6-8-6 syllables, ABCB), then sing it to "Amazing Grace" to check the fit.
8. **Subtraction edit** — take a busy chorus lyric and cut it to its 4 strongest words. That's probably the real hook.
9. **Prompt A/B test** — generate the same song idea twice: once with only adjectives ("sad emotional pretty"), once with mode+BPM+timbre+delivery. Compare how much closer the second lands.
10. **The turn** — write a song where the bridge recontextualizes the chorus (the "you" was dead the whole time / the celebration is a funeral). Make the last line detonate.
